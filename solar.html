The Sun

Sun

The star at the center of the solar system has a diameter of 1.4 million kilometers, or 109 times the diameter of the Earth. The Sun has a mass 333,000 times greater than the Earth, but has a density of only 0.25 times that of the Earth, because it is composed of 74% hydrogen, 25% helium, and 1% other elements.

Mercury

Mercury

The closest planet to the Sun, it is a small planet and has no moons. Mercury's interior consists of a large iron core with a radius of about 1,800 to 1,900 kilometers, surrounded by a silicate layer (similar to Earth's core being surrounded by a mantle and crust) which is only 500 to 600 kilometers thick. Some parts of the core may still be molten.

Mercury has no atmosphere. It is very close to the Sun, so daytime temperatures can reach 430 °C (133 °F), but at night the temperature drops to only -180 °C (-410 °F). The day-night temperature difference is 610 °C.

Earth

Earth

The only planet in the solar system with an environment that is conducive to life. Earth is the third largest and the third largest planet. Earth is a flat sphere with an average radius of 6,371 kilometers.

The interior structure of Earth consists of an iron inner core with a radius of about 1,200 kilometers. Surrounded by an outer core that is liquid (liquid) made of iron and nickel with a thickness of about 2,200 kilometers. Next up is the mantle, which is a soft, flexible solid (plastic) made of iron, magnesium, silicon and other elements with a thickness of about 3,000 kilometers. The crust is solid (solid) composed mostly of feldspar and quartz (silicon dioxide).

Mars

Mars

The fourth planet from the Sun. Mars has a diameter of about 0.5 times that of Earth. Mars has an internal structure consisting of a solid core with a radius of about 1,700 kilometers, surrounded by a molten mantle about 1,600 kilometers thick and a solid crust like Earth.

Mars is red because its surface is made up of iron oxide (iron rust). The surface of Mars is covered with many canyons. The largest canyon is called Valles Marineris, which is 4,000 kilometers long, 600 kilometers wide and 8 kilometers deep. Mars also has the tallest volcano in the solar system, Mount Olympus, which is 25 kilometers high and has a base that extends over a radius of 300 kilometers.

Jupiter

Jupiter

The fourth brightest celestial object in the sky after the Sun, the Moon and Venus, and has been known since prehistoric times. Jupiter was first explored by the Pioneer 10 spacecraft in 1973, followed by Pioneer 11, Voyager 1, Voyager 2, Ulysses and Galileo.

Jupiter is a gas giant with a dense atmosphere composed primarily of 90% hydrogen and 10% helium, with traces of methane, water and ammonia. Deep beneath it lies an outer mantle made of liquid hydrogen and helium, and an inner mantle made of metallic hydrogen and a solid rocky core about twice the size of Earth.

Saturn

Saturn

A planet known since before history. Galileo first observed Saturn with a telescope in 1610.

Saturn was observed by Pioneer 11 in 1979, followed by Voyager 1, Voyager 2, and Cassini in 2004. Saturn's atmosphere is 75% hydrogen, 25% helium, mixed with water, methane, and a small amount of ammonia. The colored bands on Saturn are caused by its very rapid rotation. This causes the atmosphere to rotate with different temperatures, resulting in alternating dark and light bands. Saturn's internal structure is similar to Jupiter's, with a solid rocky core. Surrounded by an inner mantle of metallic hydrogen and an outer mantle of liquid hydrogen and helium.

Uranus

Uranus

First discovered by William Herschel in 1991, two hundred years later, Voyager 2 surveyed Uranus in 1986 and found that its atmosphere is 83% hydrogen, 15% helium, and 2% methane. Uranus is blue because methane absorbs red and reflects blue. The atmosphere is very windy, and deep in the core, it is covered with liquid hydrogen. While most planets have an axis of rotation that is nearly perpendicular to the plane of the ecliptic, Uranus's axis is nearly parallel to the ecliptic, so temperatures are higher near the poles than near the equator. Like the outer planets, Uranus has rings. The rings are not very bright, because they are made up of small particles. They range in size from a speck of dust to 10 meters in diameter. Uranus has at least 27 moons. The largest, spherical moons include: Miranda, Ariel, Umbriel, Titania, and Oberon.

Neptune

Neptune

was discovered because astronomers found that the position of Uranus in its orbit around the Sun did not follow Newton's laws, so they hypothesized that there must be another planet farther away that disturbed Uranus' orbit. Neptune was finally discovered by Johann Galle in 1846.

Neptune has a similar composition to Uranus, i.e. it has an atmosphere of hydrogen and helium and some methane, which is why it is blue. Neptune is slightly smaller than Uranus but denser, with a solid core and about the same size as our Earth. When Voyager 2 approached Neptune, it photographed the Great Dark Spot in the southern hemisphere, which is almost half the size of Jupiter's Great Red Spot (about the same diameter as Earth). This Great Dark Spot is a tornado, like the Great Red Spot on Jupiter. Has the strongest winds in the solar system Wind speeds of 300 meters/second or 1,080 kilometers per hour
